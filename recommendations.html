Recommendations

Getting the most out of
your time and effort

Use Captrieve! For those of us who need an external memory aid, it's fantastic. (See the Science page for how this has been validated.)

Here are some things that studies have shown to have a significant positive impact on memory, from most benefit to less but still valuable.

Test yourself, instead of re-reading. Trying to recall something, and only then checking whether you got it, builds far more durable memory than going over it again. It is the single strongest item here, and it is what Captrieve does when it holds back a recurring capture and asks what are some things you associated with this cue? before showing you the answer.

Handle a thought for its meaning at the moment it arrives. The more deeply you process something, what it connects to, why it matters, where you will use it, the longer it lasts. Deciding when and where a captured thought will matter to you is exactly that kind of processing, which is why setting a good cue is itself an act of remembering.

Put it in your own words rather than copying someone else's. Material you produce yourself is remembered better than the identical material handed to you to read. A spoken capture is this by construction. You are generating the thought, not transcribing it.

Space your review out over time. Coming back to something a few times at widening intervals beats cramming it all at once, and the longer you want to keep it, the wider those intervals should grow.

Sleep. Memory is consolidated while you sleep, not only while you study. A protected night does more for what you learned today than another hour of effort would.

Make if-then plans for the things you mean to do. Turning a vague intention into a concrete when this happens, I will do that reliably improves follow-through. A Captrieve cue is precisely this, set once and then honored for you.

By contrast, here are some ideas for improving memory that are popular, but that research has not shown to be beneficial.

Brain-training games that promise to upgrade your memory. People reliably get better at the games, but the evidence that this carries over to remembering in real life is weak. The gains stay inside the game. One well-known maker of these games was fined two million dollars for advertising benefits it could not support.

Matching how you study to your "learning style." The popular idea that you will remember more when material is delivered in your preferred style, whether visual, auditory, or something else, has been tested directly and does not hold up.

Re-reading and highlighting. Both feel productive, and that feeling is the trap. Fluency is not the same as retention, and passing over the same lines again builds far less durable memory than trying to recall them would.

Supplements sold for memory. Ginkgo biloba is the cleanest example. The largest trials, run over several years, found it no better than a placebo for memory or for slowing cognitive decline.

Playing audio while you sleep to learn new material. Sleep consolidates what you already took in while awake, but you do not absorb new content from a recording played while you are asleep.

The "Mozart effect," and passive listening in general. Having music, or anything else, on in the background does not lay down memory on its own. What you do with the material is what counts, not what happens to be playing near you.
