Your phone can already store a note or pop up a reminder at a time and place. But you don't reach for those to catch fleeting thoughts, or rarely do, because they aren't fit for it. The thought lands in a pile of unsorted notes, and the reminder fires at a clock time or a general location that misses the moment when it would have been most helpful. Leaving work, sure, but not when you slip out at noon for the gym, only when you leave after 5 and you're actually headed home.

Captrieve makes a thought reappear exactly when and where you tell it to:

  • The riff you hummed, played back to you the moment you tap on your instrument's case
  • The point to raise not just somewhere at work, but specifically in the meeting with Liza and Dan
  • Being reminded of the new bedtime habit you want to develop, just as you are about to get into bed
  • The racing thoughts, captured so you can finally go to sleep, confident they will be handed back to you in the morning

Here's the strange part. Bring a creative thought to a clean conclusion and it gets harder to hold, not easier. Once it feels complete, no longer open, no longer pending, your mind wants to let it go. Captrieve catches it before it does, and retrieves it when you say you will need to have it back.

With the optional Connected plan, the right thought can reach someone else at the right moment too. Send your own voice to your anxious child during the school day, right there on his smartwatch, telling him you love him and you're proud of him. Or keep a light, respectful tie to an aging parent. Leave reminders in your own voice that reach her right when they're useful, see that her scheduled ten o'clock medication reminder went out, and let her phone quietly note that she's up in the morning and that she headed out and came home as usual. A day that stays silent then becomes your cue to call and check in.

Why not just get an app that helps you train your memory? The research says that brain-training games make you better at the games. Whether the gains cross over into real life is a live scientific debate. In early 2016, the FTC settled with a well-known brain-game maker over ads promising exactly that real-life transfer without scientific evidence to back it up. A $50 million judgment was arrived at, and then cut to $2 million because the company couldn't pay the $50 million.

You have already tried many times to have a better memory. If that was working really well for you, you wouldn't be reading this. What you need is an external memory that actively gives things back when and where you can act on them.

What's actually happening

You've had a smartphone for years. The idea you had in the shower is still gone.

The question isn't whether your phone is capable. It's whether you're actually capturing and retrieving things reliably right now. For most people, the honest answer is no.

The voice memo you'll get to later

It's in a pile of other voice memos. Unlabeled. Unindexed. Waiting for you to remember it exists and go through them all to possibly find it. Later never comes, or comes too late, or takes too long.

The note that goes nowhere

You captured it. It went into a pile. The moment when it would have been useful passed while it was sitting there. Capture without retrieval is just not admitting you are going to forget anyway.

The reminder you dismissed

It fired at 2 P.M. on Tuesday. You were in the middle of something. You dismissed it. 2 P.M. Tuesday was never the right moment anyway. The pharmacy was the right moment, and the reminder was never going to know that.

On iPhones

Geofencing is semi-reliable (which means not reliable) and gets worse as your battery level goes down. Alternative indicators of location such as connecting to a particular WiFi network help, but require you to take steps like hand-creating a Shortcut, something confusing and time consuming that the vast majority of users never do. When geofencing does work, Captrieve lets you do things like when I leave work AND it's after 5, but not if I leave work for lunch, for an off-site meeting, or to take a short break and go on a walk. Does Apple give you that without Captrieve? No.

On Android

In late 2025, Google retired location-based reminders from the tools it builds into Android, folding Reminders into Google Tasks, which has no location option. Out of the box, Google's reminders can no longer fire when you reach a place. A few third-party apps still do it, but most people never go hunting for one.

Who is Captrieve for?

People who forget things (everyone)

  • "Why can't I remember to make that call when I am at home in the evening?"
  • "Oh yeah, I promised I would go to Laura's game on Saturday, and to Michael's play next Friday. I have to put those things in my calendar when I get home."
  • "I must tell Klaus about the new direction I want to take when we meet with the Board."
  • "I can't forget again! We're out of triple-A batteries, and low on toilet paper. Stop at Target on the way home."

In fact, you can do these things without Captrieve, but do you even know how? Are you having Siri or Alexa make reminders for you, but then having them show up when it's still not a good time to act on them? Bottom line: Are you getting them done?

Creative people

Inspiration strikes when it strikes. Captrieve captures it at the speed it arrives and delivers it back when you are positioned to use it – at the studio, at the desk, at the piano, in the setting where the work happens.

People with ADHD

The thought was there and then it was just gone. You wrote it down but never looked at the note. What note? Is there a note somewhere? The reminder fired at 2 P.M. on Tuesday and you dismissed it because you were in the middle of something else. And now it's gone again.

Captrieve is different from a reminder because it fires in context – when you walk into the pharmacy, when you sit down at the piano, when you get in the car. The context provides a reminder at the time you can actually use it.

What this sounds like in practice

I was working on a new piece of music for my band, and really struggling with the bridge. It came to me on a walk, and it was really good! I hummed it into Captrieve and told it to cue me when I got to my piano. When I sat down at the piano, there it was, instead of me not being able to recall it from memory! I don't know how I worked before this.

My daughter used to worry that I'd forget my questions at the doctor and just say everything was fine. Now I capture them when I think of them, and when I walk into the doctor's office, my phone reminds me of all my questions. Last visit I remembered to ask about a medication reaction, and it turns out that it mattered.

As a person with ADHD, ideas fly in and out of my mind all the time. Now I use Captrieve with NFC tags all over my house – in the kitchen, on my guitar, at my desk, on my TV. It not only reminds me of the thing I need to do, the song I want to learn to play, the show I heard about and want to watch, but the habit of tapping an NFC tag in all of these places is actually training me to remember stuff on my own, just from being in those places!
