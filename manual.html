Manual

How it works,
in your hands.

Captrieve is built around two moments and almost nothing in between: the instant a thought arrives, and the instant it becomes useful. This manual follows that shape.

This is a living document. It grows alongside the app. Sections marked as still being written describe behavior that is settled in the design but not yet final in the built screens, and they will be completed as those screens are built.

The two moments

Capture, then retrieve

A capture is a thought you do not want to lose and cannot act on right now. A cue is what brings it back, at the moment it will be actionable. You do the work once, at capture. After that, the remembering is no longer your job.

Nothing is ever trapped behind a cue that has not fired. The inbox is always visible, so you can browse, search, and surface anything by hand at any time. There is no algorithm deciding when you are ready.

Capture

Capturing a thought

The thought is fleeing, so the app gets out of the way. The fastest way in is your voice: Speak the thought, and transcription happens in the background while the original audio is kept as a backup. You are never blocked waiting for it.

You can also capture by typing, or attach a photo when the thought is something you need to see again. Whatever the form, the next step is the same. You decide when and where you will want it back.

Cues

Setting a cue

Setting a cue is not only a delivery instruction. Answering where and when will this matter to me? is part of how the thought gets remembered in the first place.

For the common cases there are one-tap quick options, such as tonight, tomorrow morning, the next time you open the app, or when you arrive at or leave a place. For anything more specific, the cue picker opens the full set of cue types below. Places you use often can be kept as saved Locations, set on a map and reused by name.

Cue types

What a cue can watch for

  • A time. A specific date and time, or a quick option like tonight or tomorrow morning.
  • Arriving at a place, or leaving one. A capture surfaces as you reach a saved Location, or as you leave it.
  • Joining a Wi-Fi network, or leaving it. Useful for places that are really defined by their network, like the office or home.
  • Connecting a Bluetooth device, or disconnecting one. For example, your car audio, or a speaker you only use in one room.
  • Plugging in the charger, or unplugging it. Often paired with a time condition, so it fires only after a certain hour.
  • Entering a Focus or Do Not Disturb mode. A capture can wait for the moment you switch into Work, or Sleep, or any Focus you choose.
  • Tapping an NFC tag. Place a tag on an object or by a door, and a tap brings the capture back.
  • The next time you open the app. For a thought you know you will want soon and trust yourself to come back for.

Still being written: which cue types are available at first release, and which arrive shortly after, depends on platform testing in progress. This section will name the first-release set once that testing is complete.

Combining cues

Conditions, delays, and more than one

Cues combine. You can add a condition, as in when I leave work, but only after 5 PM. You can add a delay, as in 20 minutes after I get home. And a single capture can carry more than one cue, so it surfaces at whichever happens first, as in the store or the fridge tag, whichever comes first.

NFC tags

Choosing tags that work

If you want to use tag cues, you supply the tags yourself. They are inexpensive and sold almost everywhere, but they are not all alike, and the wrong ones simply will not work with Captrieve. A little care at the moment of purchase saves a frustrating afternoon later.

Buy the common kind labeled NTAG213, NTAG215, or NTAG216, and described as rewritable (some listings say writable or programmable). Any of the three works, so the least expensive is fine. A pack of round stickers is the easiest way to start, and a single pack is usually more tags than one person needs.

A warning, because this is where people get caught. Some tags on sale will not work with the app, and the product listing will not always make that obvious. Avoid the following.

  • Privacy, anti-tracking, or random ID tags. These deliberately change their identity on every tap, so the app cannot recognize them as the same tag twice.
  • Magic or changeable UID tags. These are sold for copying other tags, and are not what you want here.
  • Older MIFARE Classic tags. Many iPhones will not read them at all.
  • Tags already marked locked or read only. A tag that cannot be set up is no use.

One note about where you put a tag rather than which to buy. A plain sticker tag will not read through metal. To place a tag on something metal, such as a filing cabinet or a car dashboard, buy the kind sold as anti-metal or on metal.

If a tag you already own refuses to set up, or is not recognized when you tap it, one of the reasons above is almost always why, and a correct tag costs very little to replace.

Quick access

Reaching Captrieve in one move

A capture is only as useful as it is fast to start, and a tag tap should feel immediate. A little setup makes a large difference, and we recommend it strongly.

If your phone or watch has an Action button, assign it to open Captrieve. On iPhone 15 Pro and later, and on Apple Watch Ultra, that button launches the app with a single press, which is the fastest way to catch a thought before it slips. Several Android phones and watches have a spare or remappable button that can be set the same way, and where they do, the same advice applies.

If your phone has no such button, put the Captrieve icon on your first home screen, within reach and without searching. The seconds saved there are often the difference between keeping a thought and losing it.

There is one honest difference between the platforms, and it is smaller than it used to be. Once a tag is set up, a tap brings your capture back on both iPhone and Android without opening the app first. On iPhone the phone shows a short banner that you tap to open the capture, and if the phone is locked it asks you to unlock first. The one time you must start a scan from inside Captrieve is when you first set up a new tag, which writes the tag and locks it so nothing can quietly change it later. The banner step on iPhone is an Apple rule rather than a Captrieve choice.

Still being written: a one-press Captrieve control for Control Center on iPhone, and for the Quick Settings panel on Android, reachable with a single swipe down from the lock screen without fully unlocking, is planned and will be documented here once it is built. Even then, the phone will still ask you to unlock before it reads a tag.

The inbox

Everything, always in reach

The inbox holds your captures and is always available. Each row shows enough of the thought to recognize it, along with a short summary of its cue. Captures whose cue has fired appear in a surfaced section, so what is ready for you right now is easy to find.

You are never dependent on a cue firing. You can browse the inbox, search it, and bring any capture forward by hand whenever you want it.

Retrieval

When a capture finds you

When a cue fires, a notification arrives and tapping it opens the capture directly. From there you act on it, defer it, or change its cue. If you do not act on a surfaced capture, it stays in the surfaced section the next time you open the app, so it is not lost.

When you defer a capture that surfaced because of a place, network, or tag, the natural deferral is back to that same context, such as the next time I arrive here, rather than to a clock.

Hearing it back

When the voice is the point

Sometimes the words are not enough, and what you want back is the recording itself. You can set any voice capture to play its original recording when it returns, instead of only showing the typed-out version.

A daughter can record "remember to take the red pill and the blue one, and remember that I love you," set it on her mother's medicine cabinet tag, and her mother hears her actual voice at the cabinet. A musician can hum a riff, set it on the guitar tag, and hear the hum back at the instrument, where a typed description would have been useless. Recording in your own voice for someone else to hear is part of the Connected sharing layer.

For something like the riff, where there are no words to type out, the capture is kept as a voice note. It shows a play button rather than text, and its recording is protected so it is never cleared to save space. Playback waits for your quiet hours, the same way notifications do.

Quiet hours

Do Not Disturb and Focus

Captrieve respects your quiet hours. A capture whose cue fires during Do Not Disturb is held rather than pushed at you, and is presented when your quiet period ends.

Practicing retrieval

The optional recall prompt

For a recurring capture you feel you are starting to internalize, Captrieve can occasionally hold the content back and ask what are some things you associated with this cue? first, revealing the answer only after your attempt. Trying to recall, rather than simply being shown, is what turns a fragile memory into a durable one.

This is always your choice, set per capture, and a miss is shown gently. See the Science page for why this works.

Your data

Privacy, in practice

Your captures stay on your phone. The one thing the app may send is anonymous diagnostics, which carry no identifier and none of your capture content, and which you can switch off with a single toggle in Settings.

You can export your data at any time. On the Connected Tier, anything you share travels end-to-end encrypted, with the keys generated on your device, so the server can read nothing. See the Privacy page for the full account.

Because your data lives on your phone, back your phone up. This is ordinary phone hygiene, but it matters here for a specific reason. A tag holds only a meaningless code, never your notes or the name you gave it, so the name and the captures exist only on your phone. With a normal backup in place (iCloud, or your Android backup), reinstalling on the same account restores everything. Without one, a lost or wiped phone loses the names and captures, and a tag you set up will still work but will come back empty, ready to be named and filled again.

Tiers

Free, Solo, and Connected

Free is the everyday app, free forever, with no account and nothing leaving your phone. You get the full capture-and-retrieve loop with no limit on retrieves, and the everyday cues used one at a time: a date or time, the next time you open the app, an NFC tap (up to five tags), and a Wi-Fi network (up to two). The limit counts tags, not captures, so one tag can hold many reminders. Solo is a one-time purchase, about ten dollars, that unlocks the power features: the rest of the cues (geofence, Bluetooth, charger, and Focus), combining cues with conditions and delays, and removal of the tag and network limits. Connected is a subscription that adds an opt-in sharing layer for families, partners, and caregivers, routed through an end-to-end encrypted backend, and it includes the Solo power features. One Connected subscription can cover up to five family members through Family Sharing.

An optional Supporter purchase lets you chip in if the app has helped you. It unlocks only a small thank-you, never a feature, and the app never walls off anything you rely on.

Two Connected actions are deliberately separate. Disconnect is immediate: It stops sharing with a person right now and removes the data shared with them. Cancel is a billing action that only stops the next renewal, with access running to the end of the period you paid for. To stop sharing now, use Disconnect. See the Pricing page for current prices.
